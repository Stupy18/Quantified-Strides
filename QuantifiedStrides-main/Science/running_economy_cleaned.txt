Improving Running Economy

Running faster while making every stride feel easier is a goal shared by
most runners. One of the key concepts behind better performance is
running economy. Running economy can be compared to the fuel efficiency
of a car. The less fuel a car uses to maintain the same speed, the more
efficient it is. In the same way, running economy refers to how much
oxygen your body needs to run at a given pace. The less oxygen required,
the more efficient and faster you can become.

The first important factor in improving running economy is consistent
training. Running more, while doing so sensibly, leads to better
performance over time. Consistently logging miles and gradually
increasing your weekly volume helps your body adapt to the demands of
running. These adaptations include a lower resting heart rate and a
lower heart rate during exercise. As a result, you can maintain faster
speeds while expending less energy. Volume and intensity should always
increase gradually. A commonly used guideline is the ten percent rule,
which suggests increasing weekly mileage by no more than ten percent.
Every three weeks or so it is also beneficial to schedule a cutback week
to allow recovery while continuing to progress without increasing the
risk of injury.

Adding variety to training is another key component of improving
efficiency. Many runners fall into the habit of running the same route
at the same pace every day. Although this routine may feel comfortable,
it is not the most effective way to develop performance. A well-rounded
training plan should include a mixture of long runs, easy recovery runs,
and faster sessions such as intervals, hill sprints, and threshold runs.
This variety exposes the body to different types of stress, encouraging
broader physiological adaptations. A useful guideline when planning
training intensity is the eighty–twenty rule. Roughly eighty percent of
running should occur at an easy conversational pace, while the remaining
twenty percent should involve higher intensity efforts. This balance
helps build a strong aerobic base while still developing the ability to
run faster and more efficiently.

Strength training and plyometric exercises also contribute significantly
to running economy. Heavy resistance exercises such as squats,
deadlifts, and lunges build raw power and recruit a large number of
muscle fibers. This allows runners to produce stronger strides with less
effort. Plyometric exercises, including box jumps, skater jumps, jump
rope, and bounding, train muscles and tendons to behave like springs
that store and release energy efficiently. Two short sessions per week
combining strength work and plyometrics for approximately thirty to
forty minutes can significantly improve power and efficiency. Strength
training also provides the additional benefit of reducing injury risk.

Running drills are another valuable tool and can easily be incorporated
into warm-ups. Drills such as A-skips, B-skips, bounding, and carioca
movements help refine running form and improve neuromuscular
coordination. Neuromuscular coordination refers to how effectively the
brain communicates with the muscles during movement. These drills only
take a few minutes and are particularly beneficial before speed workouts
when efficient mechanics are essential. The focus should always be on
performing each drill with controlled and precise movement rather than
rushing through them.

Breathing control also plays an important role in efficient running.
During harder efforts, breathing can easily become chaotic. Structured
breathing patterns can help maintain rhythm and control. One commonly
used method is the two-two breathing pattern, where runners inhale for
two steps and exhale for two steps. Another option is the three-two
pattern, which involves inhaling for three steps and exhaling for two.
The advantage of the three-two pattern is that the exhale alternates
between feet, distributing impact forces more evenly and improving
balance. During easy running, breathing through the nose for both
inhalation and exhalation can help regulate effort and maintain
relaxation, while mouth breathing can be introduced as intensity
increases.

Cadence, or the number of steps taken per minute, is another factor that
influences running economy. If stride length becomes too long, energy is
wasted through overstriding. If strides are too short, fatigue can
increase due to excessive turnover. Most efficient runners fall within a
cadence range of approximately 170 to 180 steps per minute, although the
ideal cadence varies between individuals. Runners should experiment
gradually. For example, someone with a cadence of 165 steps per minute
might try increasing it by three to five percent rather than immediately
targeting a much higher number. Focusing on lighter and quicker steps
can help the body naturally adapt to a more efficient rhythm.

Vertical oscillation is another element to consider. Vertical
oscillation refers to how much the body moves up and down while running.
Some vertical movement is natural, but excessive bouncing wastes energy
because energy is directed upward rather than forward. Reducing vertical
oscillation can be achieved by maintaining a slight forward lean that
originates from the ankles rather than the waist. This posture shifts
momentum forward, shortens ground contact time, and encourages quicker
turnover.

Running form as a whole also has a substantial impact on efficiency.
Maintaining a tall posture with a straight line from head to toe helps
prevent unnecessary tension. Many runners benefit from imagining a
string pulling them upward from the crown of the head while keeping the
shoulders relaxed and the gaze forward. Foot strike should occur beneath
the body’s center of gravity in order to avoid overstriding. Arm
movement should remain compact and efficient, with elbows bent at
approximately ninety degrees and swinging smoothly backward and forward
without crossing the body excessively. Relaxation is equally important.
Runners should avoid clenching their fists or raising their shoulders,
as unnecessary tension wastes energy.

Proper fueling and hydration are essential for maintaining efficiency.
The body functions like an engine that requires appropriate fuel.
Nutrition and hydration influence how effectively muscles can utilize
oxygen. Before a run, consuming a small snack containing simple
carbohydrates, such as a banana or oatmeal, helps replenish glycogen
stores. During longer runs or races, energy gels, sports drinks, or
carbohydrate chews help the body learn to absorb and use carbohydrates
while exercising. Hydration should also be maintained by drinking water
regularly and supplementing with electrolytes when conditions are hot or
humid. Effective fueling strategies prevent energy depletion and help
the body sustain efficient performance.

Finally, advancements in footwear technology can also improve running
economy. Carbon-plated racing shoes have been shown to enhance
performance by approximately one to four percent. These shoes combine a
rigid carbon plate with lightweight, responsive foam. The plate
increases stiffness in the sole, reducing energy loss when the foot
strikes the ground, while the foam stores and returns energy with each
step. Although these shoes are not a substitute for consistent training,
they can provide a measurable performance advantage.

In summary, running economy refers to the ability to run faster while
using less energy. It can be improved through consistent training,
varied workouts, strength and plyometric exercises, running drills,
controlled breathing, optimized cadence, reduced vertical oscillation,
efficient running form, proper fueling and hydration, and appropriate
footwear. By experimenting with these strategies and applying them
consistently, runners can gradually become more efficient and ultimately
perform at higher levels while feeling more comfortable at previously
challenging paces.
