Easy Running: The Most Important Part of Your Training

One of the topics I discuss with athletes all the time is proper easy running.

It is the bedrock of endurance training and the majority of what runners actually do. Yet despite its importance, there are a lot of misconceptions about it. Different systems use different terminology, and that confusion can make it difficult for runners to understand what “easy” really means.

So I want to break everything down and give you a clear understanding of what easy running actually is, why it matters physiologically, how much you should be doing, the biggest mistakes runners make, and how to find your correct easy pace.

Why Easy Running Matters

Easy running is the foundation of endurance training.

From Arthur Lydiard’s early discoveries to Jack Daniels’ scientific training models, the message has remained consistent: easy running is not optional. It is the base layer that everything else builds upon.

If you are training for a marathon or any endurance event, workouts and race performances only work because they are supported by a large amount of easy running.

Without that base, the rest of the system falls apart.

What “Easy Running” Actually Means

Easy running is not just shuffling or jogging slowly without purpose.

It is a controlled, steady effort where breathing is relaxed, you can hold a conversation comfortably, and the effort feels sustainable.

From a physiological standpoint, easy running generally occurs around 59–74% of VO₂ max, around 60–70% of maximum heart rate, and below your first lactate threshold.

Many different training models describe this same intensity in different ways.

In Daniels Running Formula, it is called E pace. In the three-zone model, it is Zone 1. In the five-zone model, it typically falls in Zones 1–2. On an RPE scale, it is generally about 1–4 out of 10. On the Borg 6–20 scale, it usually sits around 8–12. In the Norwegian lactate model, it tends to correspond roughly to 0.7–1.5 mmol/L lactate.

The terminology varies widely, but the physiological zone is essentially the same.

The important point is that it does not matter which system you use as long as you stay in the correct intensity range.

Why Running Easy Makes You Faster

Running easy produces several powerful physiological adaptations.

First, it increases capillary density. That means your body builds more tiny blood vessels in your muscles, which improves oxygen delivery, nutrient transport, and waste removal. Think of it like building more highways in a busy city so traffic can move more efficiently.

Second, easy running stimulates mitochondrial growth. Mitochondria are the energy-producing structures inside your cells, and easy running helps increase both their number and function. That means better fuel efficiency, delayed fatigue, and a greater ability to sustain work over time.

Third, it improves the endurance function of your slow-twitch muscle fibers. These are the fibers most responsible for long-distance performance. Easy running strengthens them and helps your body rely on them more effectively.

Fourth, it improves fuel utilization. Easy running helps your body use fat more efficiently as fuel, which helps spare glycogen and reduces the chance of bonking in longer races.

You Do Not Need to Run Hard to Get Fit

A common misconception is that runners must constantly push hard in order to improve.

That is not true.

Hard workouts are important, but they only work if you have the aerobic base first.

Easy running builds that base.

Think of it like depositing money into a bank account. Every easy run adds to the balance that you eventually spend on race day.

How Much Easy Running Do You Need?

This is one of the questions athletes ask most often.

The honest answer is that it depends.

It depends on your training history, injury history, weekly volume, available training days, and goal race distance.

Broadly speaking, though, most successful endurance athletes do about 65–80% of their training at easy intensity.

This aligns with the well-known 80/20 model, where the majority of training is done at low intensity and a smaller portion is reserved for moderate and high-intensity work.

More Easy Running Means Better Durability

Interestingly, more training volume often leads to better durability, not worse durability, as long as that volume is built gradually and appropriately.

This seems counterintuitive at first, but research consistently shows that runners who build volume properly often become more resilient and less injury-prone over time.

One reason is simple: when you run more mileage, you must usually run slower in order to sustain it. That slower pace reduces stress on the body while still building fitness.

Over time this creates greater resilience, better recovery between workouts, and a higher training capacity.

The Biggest Mistake Runners Make

The most common mistake I see is runners doing their easy runs too fast.

This creates what many coaches would call junk miles.

These runs are too fast to function as recovery and too slow to count as quality work. They create fatigue, reduce workout quality, and increase injury risk while also failing to provide the intended aerobic benefit.

How to Find Your Easy Pace

The best approach is to triangulate between three things: pace, heart rate, and perceived effort.

First, use a tool like Daniels’ VDOT calculator to determine training paces based on a recent race result. The key is to use your current fitness, not your aspirational goal fitness.

Second, use heart rate as a guide. It can be useful, but it should not be treated like gospel because it is highly influenced by heat, sleep, caffeine, stress, and hydration.

Third, rely on perceived effort. For many runners, this is actually the most reliable method once it is properly calibrated. Easy running should feel conversational, controlled, and sustainable.

Expect Your Easy Pace to Change

Your easy pace will not be exactly the same every day.

Some days you will naturally be on the quicker end of the range, and some days you will be slower.

That is normal.

After hard workouts, long runs, heat, or poor sleep, your easy pace may slow down. After strides or lighter days, you may feel more pop.

The goal is not to force the same pace every day. The goal is to stay within the correct effort zone for the purpose of the session.

Common Questions

What if it feels too slow?

That is usually a sign that you are doing it correctly. Most runners are simply accustomed to running easy days too fast.

What if my run group runs faster?

Stick to your pace. Training is about building fitness, not showing it off. Save the fitness display for race day.

Should I care about heart rate?

Yes, but not obsessively. Heart rate can be useful as a guide or as a retrospective check after the run, but it should not override pace and perceived effort.

Can I run too much easy mileage?

Generally speaking, no, not in the sense most runners worry about. Easy running is the safest type of volume. It still needs to be balanced with workouts, but most runners are far more likely to do too little easy running than too much.

The Bottom Line

Easy runs are not filler.

They are the most important runs in your training program.

They build endurance, efficiency, muscular resilience, and injury resistance.

The formula for long-term progress is simple: run easy, run often, and respect the purpose of each session.

If you do that consistently over months and years, improvement becomes much more likely.

The next time you head out the door for an easy run, do not fight it and do not feel guilty about going slow. You are building the foundation that lets you run faster when it matters most.
