Golden Hour Adventures — Cleaned Transcript

Website Frustrations and Opening Banter

The conversation begins with frustration about how difficult a certain website is to navigate. The speaker complains that the system is confusing and unintuitive, especially when trying to track points or understand how the platform works after changes were made to it. The site is described as unnecessarily complicated and irritating to use.

After this opening complaint, the podcast introduction begins. Golden Hour Adventures is presented as a show about everyday people doing extraordinary things, telling stories that include both victories and defeats. Justin and Robbie introduce the episode and explain that, for now, it is just the two of them again. They reassure listeners that guests will return after Cowboy, and they hint that they already have a strong lineup planned. For the moment, however, they are focused on preparing for a major event and continuing the podcast rather than pausing it altogether.

Shoe Frustrations and the Hoka Mafate 5

The discussion quickly shifts to shoes. Robbie brings up his dissatisfaction with the Hoka Mafate 5, explaining that he had switched to it after wearing previous models for years. In his opinion, the new version is a major downgrade. He says that compared to the Mafate Speed 4, the fifth version feels significantly worse and does not inspire the same confidence, especially on technical terrain.

He explains that the increased drop is noticeable to him because he is used to a lower-drop shoe. He also complains that the shoe feels bulkier and higher off the ground, which affects stability. The laces are described as excessively long, and the tongue is criticized for being thin, short, and lacking padding. When he tightens the shoe, the laces dig into the top of his foot because the tongue does not provide enough protection. He also feels that the shoe runs larger than previous Hokas of the same size.

According to Robbie, the slightly taller lugs and the higher overall platform change how the shoe behaves on descents. Whereas he felt comfortable simply pointing downhill and trusting his foot placement in the earlier model, the new version makes him second-guess every line choice. He says the mental fatigue of constantly having to be more careful would be exhausting over a long technical race. In his view, the shoe may work fine for smoother races such as Cowboy, but it does not feel like a true mountain ultra shoe despite being marketed that way.

Reflections on Hoka and Other Shoe Brands

Justin responds by saying that Hoka shoes have never worked well for him. Every time he has tried them, they have caused discomfort in his feet or legs. He notes that he has tried several models, including different versions of the Speedgoat, but still has never found a good match. Robbie reflects on Hoka’s long history of redesigning shoes that were already working well. He mentions earlier models he loved, such as the Rapa Nui and the Speedgoat up through version four, and expresses frustration that companies keep changing successful shoes in ways that alienate long-time users.

This leads into a broader conversation about other trail shoe brands. The hosts note that Tom Evans had recently won UTMB wearing ASICS, which surprised them because they had not even realized ASICS had become a major player in trail running. They comment that both first and second place had been run in ASICS, suggesting that the brand entered the trail scene aggressively and successfully. They also mention New Balance trail shoes and discuss how rocker geometry and high responsiveness can be helpful in some contexts but may feel unstable or awkward on technical terrain.

Finding the Right Fit and the Appeal of Mountain to Coast

The hosts then discuss what kind of shoe characteristics they prefer. Robbie says he likes something that sits between plush and responsive, with enough cushion to be protective but enough responsiveness to still feel controlled. Justin asks whether he has looked into Mountain to Coast shoes, specifically the T1. Robbie says he has heard people mentioning them but has not tried them himself.

Justin shares positive experiences with Mountain to Coast’s road model, the R1, saying it is one of the most comfortable shoes he has ever worn and that he has gotten more mileage out of it than almost any other shoe. He says he has heard consistently good things about the T1 as well, including that it has a relatively low drop, moderate stability, and a weight that sounds appropriate for Robbie’s preferences. They also mention a snow-specific model and another upcoming model called the H1, which sounds more suitable for smoother trails and everyday trail use.

By the end of the discussion, Robbie sounds interested in trying the T1, even though he worries it may be late in the game to switch shoes before Cowboy. He says he had considered stockpiling old Mafate Speed 4s, but since they are already disappearing from retailers, it may be smarter to move on and find a new shoe rather than prolong dependence on a discontinued model.

Other Shoe Experiences and Preferences

The conversation continues with other shoes that have worked or failed for them. Justin says that the Brooks Caldera 6 was one of the best shoes he had used, while the later versions lost the feel that made it special. He explains that design changes such as the addition of a rock plate made the shoe stiffer and less enjoyable for his feet. He also talks about liking the Brooks Cascadia and being curious about newer versions after a slight change in drop.

They then reflect more generally on how much shoe drop affects feel. Robbie explains that once someone adapts to low-drop or zero-drop shoes, even a modest increase can suddenly feel extreme. They joke about shoes that end up sitting unused on a shelf and becoming nothing more than walking-around shoes.

UTMB and Ruth Croft’s Triple Crown

The discussion then shifts to UTMB. Justin brings up Ruth Croft, describing her as a Kiwi who has now completed a kind of Triple Crown by winning OCC, CCC, and UTMB. The hosts note that although Ruth Croft is certainly an elite and respected runner, she was not necessarily the first name many people predicted to win outright. They emphasize how significant her accomplishment is, including the fact that she became the first New Zealander to win UTMB.

Justin describes watching the livestream as she entered Chamonix and says the moment was made even better by a commentator from New Zealand who was so excited that his enthusiasm almost overshadowed Croft’s own reaction. He describes the commentary as funny, emotional, and memorable.

UTMB Coverage and Website Navigation

The hosts then talk about UTMB’s media coverage and online platform. Although they continue to complain that the website is confusing and difficult to navigate, they also admit that its functionality is impressive. Justin explains that while watching the livestream, he discovered that the video player could minimize while he explored the site. He was then able to look at the leaderboard, follow runners on the map, and even search individual names.

He says that one of the most impressive features was the ability to watch aid station footage of specific runners simply by searching for them. He tried typing in names such as Hayden Hawks and Dakota Jones and was amazed to find clips of those runners passing through checkpoints. In his opinion, this level of race coverage is far ahead of what is common in the United States.

At the same time, both hosts return to the idea that UTMB’s website is still a nightmare to navigate. They joke that maybe French engineers are simply operating at a different level and that Americans are the ones failing to understand the interface. Even so, they remain frustrated by how hard it is to locate basic information such as which race a given athlete is actually running, especially since UTMB now includes many different events across the week.

The Culture and Atmosphere of UTMB

The conversation expands from coverage to the overall culture surrounding UTMB. Justin asks Robbie what he thinks about the crowds at UTMB compared with what they typically see at American races. Robbie explains that he finds the European mountain-running culture deeply impressive. In his view, the support there feels more authentic and more rooted in the history of the sport. Spectators hike into remote mountain locations, stand in harsh conditions, and cheer passionately because they genuinely care about the race and the athletes.

He contrasts that with some American races, where spectators are often family and friends of runners who may not really understand ultra running or the demands of the course. He says this difference creates a very different feeling. In Europe, the support seems to come from a broader cultural appreciation of mountain sport, whereas in the United States it can sometimes feel more like isolated personal support rather than collective enthusiasm.

Justin agrees and says that the crowds are one of the things that make UTMB so special. He imagines that the towns must benefit enormously from the event and that the atmosphere likely stays energetic throughout the race, not just for elites. Both hosts say they would love to race UTMB one day, although they also mention that simply going to the area to do a multi-day running trip with hotels and long mountain days would be incredibly appealing.

The hosts briefly revisit the controversy from a few years ago surrounding UTMB’s sponsorships and qualifying system. Beginner Trail Running Advice

After the UTMB discussion, the podcast shifts into a practical advice segment aimed at beginner trail runners. The hosts frame themselves as experienced trail runners wanting to help newcomers make a smoother transition into the sport. They encourage listeners to subscribe, leave reviews, and support the podcast, but then move into the actual advice.

The first tip is to slow down. They explain that pace on trails is not the same as pace on the road. Robbie says that when he first transitioned from concrete and bike-path running to trails, he made the mistake of assuming he could run the same pace. He quickly learned that climbing, uneven terrain, and reduced footing make that unrealistic. Trail running requires a different rhythm and mindset, and trying to force road pace on technical trails is a good way to become discouraged or overworked.

Justin reinforces this with examples from his own runs. He describes outings where a twenty-mile trail run took five or even six hours because of overgrown sections, steep climbing, rock gardens, and difficult descents. He emphasizes that trail runners need to think more in terms of time on feet than in terms of pace. Someone coming from the road might assume that a familiar distance will take only slightly longer on the trail, when in reality the same mileage could take double the time depending on terrain.

Nutrition and Gut Preparation for Trail Running

The second beginner tip is nutrition. The hosts say this is one of the most important and most misunderstood differences between road racing and trail or ultra running. Robbie explains that even though a trail runner may be moving more slowly, the intensity on climbs and technical terrain can be very high, which changes fueling needs substantially. In his view, trail runners often need more calories and more carbohydrates than they expect, particularly if the course involves sustained climbing or long durations.

They also point out that trail racing tends to rely more on real food than road racing. Whereas road events may mainly offer water, sports drink, and perhaps a banana or gel, trail races often involve a much wider variety of food. Robbie advises newer trail runners to carry extra food and to be willing to experiment with more substantial fueling options such as candy, snacks, or other real foods.

Justin adds that runners need to prepare their gut by practicing with food during training. A fifty-kilometer trail race can last far longer than a marathon, which changes what the stomach must handle. He notes that runners should not assume that because something worked in training it will always work on race day, so having multiple options is smart. He mentions examples such as Rice Krispie treats, fruit strips, and even a new product consisting of mashed potatoes in a resealable pouch. He says this kind of food can be practical and effective during long efforts and encourages runners to experiment before race day.

Picking Lines on Trail

The third tip is learning how to pick lines. The hosts explain that this is an underappreciated skill in trail running. Picking a line means identifying the safest and most efficient path for foot placement through rocks, roots, mud, and other obstacles. On technical terrain, a runner cannot simply put a foot down without thinking. Every step requires awareness.

Justin explains that when runners fail to pick their lines, they are more likely to clip a rock, catch a root, or fall. He emphasizes that this skill matters not only when descending quickly but also when hiking uphill. Even slower movement benefits from deliberate foot placement. Trail running is not just about fitness; it also involves movement skill, terrain reading, and constantly adapting to what is underfoot.

Closing Summary

As the episode wraps up, Justin summarizes the three beginner tips. First, trail runners should slow down and accept that trail pace will not match road pace. Second, they should learn how to fuel properly, including practicing with real food and preparing the gut for long efforts. Third, they should develop the ability to pick lines so they can move more safely and efficiently across technical terrain.

He closes by inviting listeners to find the podcast on Apple Podcasts, Spotify, YouTube, and other platforms, ending with the same casual and humorous tone that carried through the episode.
